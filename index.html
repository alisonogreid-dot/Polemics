The Art of Polemic

When stories take sides — and why that's a rhetorical choice, not a flaw

01What is a Polemic?

Polemic
From Greek πολεμικός (polemikos) — "warlike", from πόλεμος (polemos) — "war"

Contentious rhetoric intended to support a specific position by forthright claims and to undermine the opposing position.

Unlike a balanced argument or persuasive essay, a polemic doesn't aim for middle ground. It picks a side, commits fully, and uses every rhetorical tool available to make the audience feel the urgency and justice of that position.

Think of it as the difference between a debate (where both sides get fair treatment) and a speech at a rally (where the speaker has already chosen a side and wants you to join them). Famous polemicists include Jonathan Swift, George Orwell, Karl Marx — and, as we'll see, Greta Thunberg.

Important Distinction

Polemic ≠ Rant. A rant is emotional without craft. A polemic is strategically one-sided — backed by evidence, structured for maximum impact, and using emotion as a rhetorical tool, not a substitute for argument. The best polemics make you feel and think.

You've already been encountering polemic throughout this unit. The Lorax is a polemic — it's entirely one-sided, it has a clear villain, and Dr. Seuss isn't interested in giving the Once-ler's economic perspective any legitimacy. Your Easter Island narratives in Week 1 were mini-polemics — you committed to one interpretation and narrated from inside it. Now we're going to name the technique and learn to use it deliberately.

02Key Characteristics

Click each characteristic to see what it means and how to recognise it.

01 One-Sided Commitment ▼

The writer or speaker has chosen a position and argues from inside it. There is no attempt at balance, no "on the other hand," no fair hearing for the opposition. This isn't accidental — it's the whole point. Acknowledging complexity would dilute the urgency.

Compare: A persuasive essay says "While there are valid concerns on both sides..." A polemic says "There is only one acceptable response."

02 Emotionally Charged Language ▼

Word choices are deliberate and designed to provoke feeling — anger, shame, urgency, moral outrage — not just inform. Where a neutral report says "impacted," a polemic says "destroyed." Where a report says "economic challenges," a polemic says "the sufferings of the many."

The emotion isn't decoration. It's the engine of the argument.

03 Clear Antagonist ▼

Polemic identifies who or what is wrong. It names the enemy — an institution, a system, a group, an ideology. Remember Lévi-Strauss and binary opposites from Week 4? Polemic thrives on binaries: the many vs the few, children vs adults, the planet vs profit. These force the audience to pick a side.

04 Direct Address ▼

Often speaks directly to the audience or to the antagonist using "you." This creates confrontation — it removes the safe distance of speaking about something in the abstract. It's the difference between "leaders have failed" and "you have failed."

05 Moral Certainty ▼

No hedging. No "perhaps" or "it could be argued." The speaker presents their position as not just correct but morally necessary. Doubt or hesitation would weaken the effect. The implication is always: anyone who disagrees is either foolish or complicit.

06 Call to Action ▼

Polemic doesn't just describe a problem — it demands change. It wants the audience to do something, feel something, or reject something. The ending is never neutral; it's a call to arms, a moral demand, or a warning.

07 Strategic Use of Evidence ▼

Evidence is selected to serve the argument, not to give a complete picture. This isn't dishonesty — it's rhetoric. A policy paper presents all the data. A polemic chooses the data that supports its case and leaves out what doesn't. Understanding what a polemic excludes is just as important as understanding what it includes.

03Mapping Thunberg's UNFCCC Speech

Greta Thunberg delivered this speech at COP24 in Katowice, Poland, in December 2018. She was 15 years old. Let's map it against the seven characteristics of polemic.

Characteristic 01

One-Sided Commitment

The speech has one position: world leaders are failing to act on climate change, and this failure is a moral choice, not a practical limitation. There is no acknowledgement that leaders face genuine trade-offs, that climate policy involves difficult economic decisions, or that progress has been made.

From the speech
"You only speak of green eternal economic growth because you are too scared of being unpopular."

She reduces world leaders' positions to a single motivation (fear of being unpopular), dismissing the possibility that they have legitimate reasons for their approach. This is a classic polemical move: simplify the opponent's position to make it easier to attack.

Characteristic 02

Emotionally Charged Language

Nearly every sentence carries emotional weight. The word choices are designed to provoke shame, guilt, and urgency — not to inform.

From the speech
"Our civilisation is being sacrificed for the opportunity of a very small number of people to continue to make enormous amounts of money."
Word / Phrase Neutral Alternative Why the Polemical Choice?
"sacrificed" affected / impacted Implies deliberate destruction — someone is choosing to destroy
"stealing their future" compromising outcomes Frames inaction as theft — a crime, not a policy failure
"sufferings of the many" negative impacts Human pain, not statistics. Echoes political rhetoric ("the many" vs "the few")
"running out of time" limited time remaining Creates panic, a ticking clock. No room for delay
Characteristic 03

Clear Antagonist

Thunberg's antagonist is not vague. It is "you" — the world leaders sitting in front of her. She also identifies a broader system: wealthy nations, rich individuals, and political cowardice.

From the speech
"It is the sufferings of the many which pay for the luxuries of the few."

Notice the binary structure — exactly what Lévi-Strauss describes. The many who suffer vs the few who profit. The children who inherit the crisis vs the adults who created it. There is no middle category. Polemic thrives on binary opposites because they force the audience to pick a side.

Characteristic 04

Direct Address

The word "you" appears repeatedly. She is not speaking about leaders in the abstract — she is speaking to them, in the room, to their faces.

From the speech
"You say you love your children above all else. And yet you are stealing their future."

The shift from "you say" to "and yet" is devastating. She quotes their own values back at them and then accuses them of betraying those values. This technique — turning the opponent's own words into evidence against them — is one of polemic's most powerful tools.

Characteristic 05

Moral Certainty

There is no hedging. No "perhaps" or "it could be argued that" or "while we acknowledge the difficulty."

From the speech
"We can not solve a crisis without treating it as a crisis."

This is framed as a self-evident truth — almost a tautology. It closes down debate by making disagreement seem absurd. If you're not treating it as a crisis, you're not solving it. The implication: anyone who disagrees is either foolish or complicit.

Characteristic 06

Call to Action

The speech doesn't end with description or analysis. It ends with a demand — and a threat.

From the speech
"We have not come here to beg world leaders to care. You have ignored us in the past and you will ignore us again. We've come here to let you know that change is coming whether you like it or not."

She refuses the role of supplicant ("not come here to beg"), predicts the audience's failure ("you will ignore us again"), and then reframes the power dynamic entirely: change is coming regardless of what leaders do. The call to action is aimed past the leaders in the room, toward everyone watching.

Characteristic 07

Strategic Use of Evidence

Thunberg uses very little data in this speech. There are no statistics, no graphs, no citations. Instead, her evidence is personal (she is 15, she will be 75 in 2078, she might have children) and moral (you say you love your children, yet you steal their future).

This is a deliberate choice. A policy speech would use data. A polemic uses narrative and moral argument because its goal is not to inform but to provoke action.

What Does This Polemic Leave Out?

This is a critical question — not because it weakens the speech, but because understanding what polemic excludes helps us understand how it works.

The speech does not mention: existing climate agreements (Paris, Kyoto), renewable energy progress, the economic constraints developing nations face, the jobs and communities that depend on carbon-intensive industries, or the genuine policy trade-offs leaders navigate.

A balanced essay would include these. A polemic deliberately doesn't — because including them would create space for the audience to feel that "progress is being made" or "it's complicated." Polemic removes that escape route.

COPVL prompts — applied to a polemic
  1. Content: What is Thunberg's central argument? What evidence does she use? (Look back at Section 3 — the UNFCCC mapping will help.)
  2. Origin: Who is Thunberg? Where and when was this delivered? Who is in the room?
  3. Purpose: What is she trying to do to her audience? (Hint: it isn't to inform.) How does identifying this as a polemic change your answer?
  4. Value: What can this source genuinely tell us — about the climate movement, about youth political voice, about how environmental arguments are made in 2019?
  5. Limitation: This is the interesting one. A polemic's limitations (one-sidedness, emotional framing, omitted evidence) are also its design. So: what does this source not tell us? And — more importantly — does that limit its value, or just its scope?

Synthesis question

In 3–4 sentences: Can a deliberately one-sided source still be a valuable source? Use Thunberg's speech to argue your position. (This is a polemical question — feel free to answer it polemically.)
